Some Important Things to Know In Your 20s

Maintaining a healthy diet helps prevent overweight and obesity, especially for 20-year-olds, who often live a college lifestyle. According to the U.S. Department of Agriculture, 72 percent of U.S. men and 64 percent of U.S. women are overweight or obese. A study published in a 2007 edition of the “Journal of the American Dietetic Association” found that college students who were more knowledgeable about nutrition guidelines had healthier eating patterns. Appropriate diets for 20-year-olds are based on gender, activity level and weight-management goals

Calories

The USDA provides calorie recommendations for all adults, including 20-year-olds. Based on these guidelines, men ages 19 to 30 require 2,400 to 2,600 calories if they are sedentary, 2,600 to 2,800 calories if they are moderately active and about 3,000 calories per day if they are active or participate in physical activity equivalent to walking more than 3 miles per day. The USDA also estimates that women ages 19 to 30 require 1,800 to 2,000 calories if they are sedentary, 2,000 to 2,200 calories if they are moderately active and about 2,400 calories per day if they are active.

Weight loss

Overweight and obese 20-year-olds with a body mass index of 25 or above may consider a reduced-calorie diet for weight loss. BMI is a measurement calculated from weight and height and is a good indicator of body fat and chronic-disease risks. To calculate your BMI, multiply your weight in pounds by 703, divide by your height in inches and divide again by your height in inches; you can also use an online BMI calculator like the one provided by the National Heart, Lung and Blood Institute. To safely and effectively lose excess body weight, the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics encourages an energy reduction of 500 to 1,00

Macronutrient Composition

A healthy diet for a 20-year-old consists of a good balance of carbohydrates, protein and fat. The Institute of Medicine recommends that adults ages 19 and older consume 45 to 65 percent of their calories from carbohydrates, 10 to 35 percent from protein and 20 to 35 percent of their daily calories from fat. The institute also encourages 20-year-old men to consume at least 130 grams of carbohydrates and 56 grams of protein each day and 20-year-old women to consume at least 130 grams of carbs and 46 grams of protein every day; pregnant and nursing women require at least 71 grams of protein each day.

Sample 2,600-Calorie Plan

A 2,600-calorie diet is often appropriate for 20-year-old men who are moderately active and some large-framed sedentary 20-year-old men. A healthy, sample USDA 2,600-calorie meal plans includes 3.5 cups of vegetables; 2 cups of fruits; 9 ounces of grains; 3 cups of dairy foods; 6.5 ounces of protein foods such as lean meats, seafood, eggs and soy products; 8 teaspoons of oils; and 362 additional calories each day.

Sample 2,000-Calorie Plan

A 2,000-calorie diet is usually appropriate for 20-year-old women who are sedentary to moderately active. A USDA sample 2,000-calorie meal plan consists of 2.5 cups of vegetables; 2 cups of fruits; 6 ounces of grains; 3 cups of dairy foods; 5.5 ounces of protein foods such as lean meats, seafood, eggs and soy products; 6 teaspoons of oils; and 258 extra calories each day.

Key Nutrients You Need To Know

Protein

Protein helps keep you full and provides the building blocks so you can make and keep muscle. "Recent studies suggest that, at a minimum, we need 60 to 70 grams of protein a day," says Leslie Bonci, M.P.H., R.D., director of sports medicine at the University of Pittsburgh Medical Center. Get your quota by eating skinless white meat poultry, lean steak, fish, eggs, beans, tofu, and low-fat dairy.

Potassium

In order for your muscles and heart to function properly, you need to consume a hefty dose of potassium. But most women in the U.S. aged 20 to 39 get less than half the recommended amount, according to the USDA. Munching on 2 cups of fruit and 2 1/2 cups of veggies daily will help you get all the potassium you need.

Omega-3 fatty acids

These polyunsaturated fatty acids may boost serotonin, a feel-good chemical in your brain that research shows may be linked to depression when transmitted in low levels. Since women are twice as likely as men to be diagnosed with depression, start incorporating salmon and tuna into your diet, which are rich in omega-3s. You can also get your fill from walnuts, ground flaxseed, and canola oil. p>

Make fast food healthy

Grabbing dinner on the go means you may be missing out on crucial nutrients, says Bonnie Taub-Dix, R.D., a spokesperson for the American Dietetic Association. Slave over the stove? Nah. Choose healthy convenience foods such as rotisserie chicken, shrimp cocktail, steamed dumplings, and salads and enjoy them with speedy additions from your kitchen such as whole wheat pasta, instant brown rice, and frozen veggies. (If you're looking for a quick homemade meal, try meal prepping.)

Drink to your health

Margaritas, mojitos, and cosmos can add up faster than you can say cheers. Enter light beer (110 calories in a 12-ounce bottle). It's filling, so you're less likely to want a second, and it takes a good, long time to drink.
